Nursing Home Neglect vs Elder Abuse

Neglect and abuse are distinct under California law. Here’s the difference between neglect and abuse—and why it matters for your case.

What Is Nursing Home Neglect? (Nursing Home Negligence)

Nursing home negligence—or neglect—is the failure to provide the care a resident needs, resulting in harm. It is usually not intentional. Examples: failing to turn a resident (causing bed sores), not providing enough food or water, ignoring infections, or not assisting with mobility so the resident falls. The facility or staff failed to act when they had a duty to care.

What Is Elder Abuse?

Elder abuse typically involves intentional harm or mistreatment—physical abuse (hitting, restraining improperly), emotional abuse (humiliation, threats), sexual abuse, or financial exploitation. In California, the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act covers both abuse and neglect and can allow for enhanced remedies (e.g., attorney fees, punitive damages) when conduct is reckless or malicious.

Emotional and Financial Neglect

Emotional neglect in nursing homes—ignoring a resident, leaving them isolated, or failing to meet psychosocial needs—can be illegal under California's Elder Abuse Act when it causes harm. Is emotional neglect in nursing homes illegal? Yes when it rises to recklessness or results in injury; a lawyer can advise on your facts.

Financial neglect or exploitation is when someone misuses a resident's money or assets (e.g., unauthorized withdrawals, forged checks). It is a form of elder abuse and can be pursued alongside neglect claims.

Why the Distinction Matters

Some cases involve only neglect; others involve both neglect and abuse. The legal theories and available damages can differ. A nursing home neglect lawyer or bed sore attorney can review your facts and advise which claims apply.
